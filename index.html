Fractals

in the Real World

by @hcz

What is this image about? It looks like a calabash or a rotated number "eight". Actually, it is Mandelbrot set, a fractal. "Fractal" is a mathematical concept to describe a shape which is similar to itself at every scale. If we zoom this image in, we can see more and more shapes like Mandelbrot set itself. It is an infinitely complicated world created from one mathematical formula. Click here to see what happens!

The main topic of this presentation is fractals. In this presentation, we are talking about different kinds of fractals and how they change our life. We will introduce the history and general concepts of fractals as well as some famous fractal forms. Then, we will see three kinds of fractals exist in the real world:

- Fractals as natural phenomena
- Fractals as tools for scientists and engineers
- Fractals as creative works

The research on fractals has a long history. In the 17th century, Gottfried Leibniz, the person on the left picture, raised the idea of "recursive self-similarity." However, he thought that only straight line can be similar to itself. Many years later, another mathematician called Karl Weierstrass realized that a more complex self-similar shape may exist. Finally, The person on the right side, Helge von Koch, created one of the first well-known fractal curve: Koch snowflake.

Then, in 1915, Waclaw Sierpinski presented Sierpinski triangle. It is a 2-D graph constructed using similar techniques as in Cantor set by Weierstrass's student Georg Cantor. It is easy to construct a Sierpinski triangle: just repeat the process that making three copies of the triangle, put them together, and construct a new triangle. Based on Cantor and Sierpinski's work, a series of fractals are developed, including Sierpinski carpet and Menger sponge.

At that time, the research on fractals was limited. Without the help of computers, it lacked effective ways to visualize them. Half a century later, with the help of new technologies, Benoit Mandelbrot, the person talking in this photo, started his research on several new kinds of fractals, including quasi-self-similar systems like Mandelbrot set. Also, he found that some phenomena in the real world are related to fractals.

How can we measure the complexity of a fractal? Felix Hausdorff, a pioneer of modern topology, gave an answer by redefining "dimensions" as the growth rate of complexity to measurement accuracy. We know that the space we live in is 3-D and cartoon characters are 2-D. But, what if the number of dimensions is not an integer? It can be a fractal.
