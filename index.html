Fractals

In the Real World

What is this image about? It looks like a calabash or a rotated number "eight". Actually, it is Mandelbrot set, a fractal. "Fractal" is a mathematical concept to describe a shape which is similar to itself at every scale. If we zoom this image in, we can see more and more shapes like Mandelbrot set itself. It is an infinitely complicated world created from one mathematical formula.
Click here to watch the animation!

The main topic of this presentation is fractals. In this presentation, we are talking about different kinds of fractals and how they change our life. We will introduce the history and general concepts of fractals as well as some famous fractal forms. Then, we will see three kinds of fractals exist in the real world:
- Fractals as natural phenomena
- Fractals as tools for scientists and engineers
- Fractals as creative works

The research on fractals has a long history. In the 17th century, Gottfried Leibniz, the person on the left picture, raised the idea of "recursive self-similarity." However, he thought that only straight line can be similar to itself. Many years later, another mathematician called Karl Weierstrass realized that a more complex self-similar shape actually exists. Finally, The person on the right side, Helge von Koch, created one of the first well-known fractal curve: Koch snowflake.

Then, in 1915, Waclaw Sierpinski presented Sierpinski triangle. It is a 2-D shape constructed using similar techniques as in Cantor set by Weierstrass's student Georg Cantor. It is easy to construct a Sierpinski triangle: just repeat the process that making three copies of the triangle, put them together, and construct a new triangle. Based on Cantor and Sierpinski's work, a series of fractals are developed, including Sierpinski carpet and Menger sponge.

At that time, the research on fractals was limited. Without the help of computers, it lacked effective ways to visualize them. Half a century later, with the help of new technologies, Benoit Mandelbrot, the person talking in this photo, started his research on several new kinds of fractals, including quasi-self-similar systems like Mandelbrot set. Also, he found that some phenomena in the real world are related to fractals.

How can we measure the complexity of a fractal? Felix Hausdorff, a pioneer of modern topology, gave an answer by redefining "dimensions" as the growth rate of complexity to measurement accuracy. We know that the space we live in is 3-D and cartoon characters are 2-D. But, what if the number of dimensions is not an integer? It can be a fractal.

Not only mathematicians, physicists paid a lot of attention to fractals too. Georg Christoph Lichtenberg, a German physicist, found that special patterns often appears on the surface of materials experienced electric discharges. He summarized these patterns as Lichtenberg figure. This is one of the first well-known examples of real-world fractals.

Romanesco, a kind of broccoli, is another example of natural fractals. John Walker, the person took this photo, inspected this vegetable using a high-resolution camera. He found the shape of Romanesco repeats in different scales. This feature is just like Mandelbrot set we mentioned before. The only different is that you can not buy a Mandelbrot set in a supermarket, but for Romanesco, you can.

Romanesco is not the only fractal plant. There is a fractal called Barnsley fern which looks like real ferns. The fractal is similar to Sierpinski triangle: one iteration is making three copies of the shape itself. Stephen Wolfram, the author of the book A New Kind of Science, found that many plants follow a fractal growth pattern.

There are much more fractals exist as natural phenomena. Some are what we usually can see like snowflakes, and some are created by chance like the "Sierpinski" shell on the left side. There is a question: do these fractal features really matter to us?

Surprisingly, one of the most influential research on fractals is about geological measurement. The picture shows that when we use shorter rulers to measure the Britain coastline, we will get a longer length. Mandelbrot published a famous paper in 1967 about this phenomenon. In fact, we need Hausdorff dimension to describe the coastline length.

A more modern application of fractals is in computer games. In the early years, game developers create 3-D terrain models by hand. It was time-consuming. But now, many of them use fractals. Here is a screenshot of a popular sandbox game called Minecraft. Everything in it including mountains, waters, trees and villages are generated by a computer, and the area of its world map is even larger than the real surface area of the earth.

Brownian motion is a statistical fractal that describes the movement of particles in a fluid. Modern scientists borrow the idea from it to analysis and predict the change of various data. With the power of statistics and big data, this technique is becoming more and more important. Nowadays, Brownian motion is used in weather forecasting, stock trading, and many other areas.

Besides science and engineering, fractals are also used in art and creative works. In this video, a group of contemporary artists created an interactive artwork using a set of cameras and projectors:

Sen Gu, a well-known science writer in China, shared his fractal creation on his blog. In this photo, he made a Sierpinski triangle using the technique of making ramen.

If you still remember Lichtenberg figure, it can be a cool decoration in your room, too. The photo shows an acrylic block with a Lichtenberg figure in it. Although it is somewhat difficult to make Lichtenberg figure at home, it is offered by sellers on Amazon and Ebay. In addition to acrylic blocks, many interesting fractal items are available there. Fractals are no longer boring mathematical concepts, but something really cool!
